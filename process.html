The process

The Beginning

The start of a project is always difficult, and for me, it was no different. I had at least 5 different project ideas until I finally decided what I wanted to do. As a member of my school's environmental club, Carlmont Green Team, we established a tri-bin system that wasn't quite working out the way we wanted it to. Student volunteers were taking out the trash because there wasn't enough funding to hand the job over to the janitors, so it wasn't the most sustainable project in the long run. Additionally, not all of the students knew how to sort their waste, so the bins were just getting contaminated. We decided that things needed to change, so we ordered big compost bins for our school, made a video to educate the student body, and moved the tri-bins to sit at the back of the school. After this entire process, trash finally started getting sorted.​

When I saw the bins just sitting there, it seemed like such a waste (no pun intended). That's when an idea sparked, and I decided to bring those tri-bins to Ralston Middle School.​

After proposing the project idea to principal Michael Dougherty, he mentioned that a tri-bin system was something many people at Ralston had wanted and agreed to work with me.

Advisor

I met my advisor, Reyna Oceguera, at one of my school's environmental meetings. She is a Waste Zero Specialist at Recology and really helped guide me through the whole process such as helping me come up with ways to educate the students, printing flyers, logistics, and more. Without her, I would have definitely struggled with the process more than I already did!

Meetings

Scheduling and having meetings with the principal was crucial in getting the ball rolling. Although emailing would appear to do the trick, this was not always the case. It took a bit of time to receive responses because of the principal's busy schedule, and I hadn't known he was on paternity leave until a few emails and weeks later. At each meeting, I would show up with questions and things that needed to be done. From there, we would discuss our next steps, which would include things that each of us needed to do. 

Volunteering

As part of the process, I recruited members of my school's Green Team to help me with some of the tri-bin work. Since the bins had been sitting outside for a couple of months, they were dirty and needed to be cleaned. When I announced this volunteer opportunity to the club, I was afraid not many people would show up because cleaning trash bins was unappealing to many. However, I was pleasantly surprised with the turnout! We got the job done within a couple of hours, and the bins were brought to Ralston by Carlmont's custodians later that week.

Implementation

When the tri-bins were brought to Ralston, I delivered some of the posters my club members and I made. Olivia Long, president of Ralston's student government, helped hang up the posters soon after the bins were brought over. Unfortunately, shortly after we began preparing to put the tri-bins out, school had to shut down due to the coronavirus. 

Nevertheless, during quarantine, I kept in contact with Mr. Dougherty and improved the sorting guide and resources. During the first quarter, students will be educated and ready for the tri-bins when it's safe to go back to school!
